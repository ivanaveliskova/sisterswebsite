Sr. Ermelinda Cordasco, C.S.JB.  -  An Educator for All Ages
 

  

 


As St. John Villa Academy celebrated its 85th Anniversary, it recognized one of its stellar faculty members  - Sister Ermelinda Cordasco, C.S.JB.

Amazingly, Sister (98 yrs.) has taught at the High School in every decade since the school opened. Although no longer in the classroom Sister still tutors on a one-to-one basis in both Italian and Spanish. 

Sister thrives on treasuring the past while enjoying the challenges of the future and delights in keeping up with family, friends and alums via her e-mail.

Sr. Placid Zinicola, C.S.JB.

 + October 11, 1919- August 22, 2013  +
May she rest in peace. Amen.

 


A Memorial Mass for Sr. Placid will be celebrated on Sunday morning, October 27, 2013
at St. John Villa Academy Chapel.  Time to be announced.
 

 


Eulogy for Sr. Placid Zinicola was delivered by Sr Lois Darold, CSJB, August 23, 2013

From her earliest years there were signs that Florence Zinicola, later  to be known as Sr. Placid, was destined to be a Sister of St. John the Baptist.  To start with, in her loving family she was a “sister” to 3 brothers and 10 sisters. What a wonderful  preparation for community living! From the 11 girls in the family, 3 entered religious communities, providing young Florence with pathways to follow. But, Florence found her own, unique religious life path  -  to the Sisters of St. John the Baptist. Judging from the beautiful picture of the child John in their home, the family had a devotion to St. John the  Baptist, so the Holy Spirit did not have to work too hard to direct the young Florence to enroll in the newly opened St. John Villa Academy.   

 There the not-yet Sister Placid straddled two worlds: a dedicated student who enthusiastically enjoyed the range of high school challenges and a Junior Postulant who was beginning to learn about religious life. At Villa Sister found a world of academic opportunities, new friends and what would be the most important gift of her life - her vocation to the Sisters of St. John the Baptist.

After her graduation, Sr Placid completed her formal religious formation and then embarked upon her first apostolic assignment, that of high school teacher at St. John Villa Academy. For twenty-four years hers was the warm and welcoming face who could make the sciences, especially chemistry, comprehensible to young minds. Beyond that, in the spirit of our Founder, Bl. Alfonso Fusco, hers were the compassionate ears and helping hands that assisted generations of young girls through their adolescent years. Whether they sought her in the classroom or in her beloved Chapel, between classes or after school, Sister was there - concerned about them and ready to offer loving support. Truly a “student advocate” before the term was popular!

Always responsive to the needs of her Community, Sister left Villa in 1963 to serve in areas of administration at Mt. St. John Academy and Alphonsus Junior College, both in New Jersey. She returned to Villa in the mid-70’s and for 17 years I was privileged to have her as my Vice-Principal, my “right hand”. I watched - and learned - as new generations of teachers, parents, and most importantly students found their way to her desk and her heart.

Like her name, Sister’s demeanor was placid and brought calmness to the frenetic activities of a school office. The source of her peacefulness was the Chapel where after school she spent hours in prayer, remembering the needs of the families and students that had crossed her path that day. She embodied the words of Jesus: “Let the little ones – and the not-so-little ones -  come to Me.” Not for herself, but so that through her they might come to Jesus.

With an energy that belied her years Sr. Placid continued her daily service at Villa. At times this energy was a 2-edged sword that caused us “to check our hearts and to pray mightly”. Sister was placid -  but also a bit stubborn. More than once, one of us would report sighting Sr Placid on the stepladder reaching up past the tabernacle to bring down the flower vases! Only her Guardian Angel kept her from coming down with the vases.

In 2011 Sister was transferred to Mt. St John Convent. Here, lovingly surrounded by her peers, some of her former students, and dedicated care-givers she engaged more deeply in   the ministry of prayer. And, she revealed a love for books befitting the voracious reader she now could be. It became a communal mission to keep Sr Placid supplied with books. And we looked forward to reading her micro-reviews when she returned them to giver!

When, earlier this month, a request was posted on FaceBook asking her friends and former students to pray for Sister in the last phase of her journey home to God, over 50 responses were posted in the first 24 hours. An amazing tribute for one who preferred the sanctuary light of the Chapel to the floodlight  of status.

So, today with tender thoughts and trust in God’s promise of eternal life, we - her family, her Sisters, her friends - bid farewell to Sr Placid:

 *  a loving “sister” to many by blood and by love;  
          *  a woman of faith; a pray-er par excellence;  
          *  the tender keeper of Jesus' house, too many years to count;  
          *  a beloved friend for all the ages. 

She has fulfilled the goal she set as a Junior Postulant:  “ If God gives me the grace to enter the novitiate where I will be able to say the prayers and observe the rules of the Community, I am sure that I will become a faithful spouse of Christ and a true follower of St. John.  With Christ I will say, “Not my will but Thine be done.”  Yes, Sister Placid, you have achieved your goal for all eternity.   “Veni, Sponsa Christi” [Come, Bride of Christ]          

 

       

 


Sr. Mary Clemens Lawrence, C.S.JB.

 + October 19, 1911- March 29, 2013  +
May she rest in peace. Amen.
       What do most people think of as they near their 51st birthday? For many, thoughts of retirement and plans of more leisurely living begin to be entertained. It is said that "God’s ways are not man’s ways". Apparently, neither is God’s timing - especially when it comes to a call to religious life!

     Sr. Mary Clemens Lawrence, C.S.JB., was born into a Scotch-Irish Catholic family in 1911. The oldest of three, she was a Brooklyn "girl" through and through. St. Michael’s Church was just down the street and in her early years the family’s social life centered around parish activities.

     Family needs forced her into the business world at a young age. Sister recalls: "I was offered good positions for the times, worked through the depression and made an enviable salary. I was able to care for my mother physically and financially until she passed away. These were all God’s gifts."

     In retrospect, it would also be God’s gift that a marriage engagement did not work out. "When all my friends were married, I wondered from time to time why God did not send someone worthwhile my way. My mother was not gone from my life but a few months, when I had a strong stirring to enter the convent. I was then 50 years of age and not in the bracket where candidates were being sought at that time"

     "I never approached the Orders of a number of friends I had in religious life and to this day cannot I tell you why, except that if God wants you somewhere, and you cooperate, you get there. I heard of the Sisters of St. John the Baptist in a roundabout way. Understanding that they had recently accepted someone my age (which turned out not to be the case), I wrote and asked for an interview. I had not mentioned my age in my letter. Because I did not look my years, the Sister I met with thought I was joking when I told her my age. But God wanted me here and so after competing the preliminary procedures I entered the Baptistine Community on January 6, 1963 - at the age of 51."

     "Since I was advanced in age and not a teacher or nurse, people wondered what I would be doing in the convent. It so happened that when I pronounced my vows there was a need for a secretary at the Provincialate. I was assigned there and have been doing this type of work for the last 38 years - even now that I am at the retirement residence! "God arranges everything. I felt sure of that from my first experience with the Sisters and still feel that way now. Thank God for all His blessings".

     At age 51 Sr. Mary Clemens put aside retirement plans, placed her future confidently in God’s hands, took a leap of faith, and accepted a commitment to a new life with the Sisters of St. John the Baptist. For over 49 years Sr. Mary Clemens fulfilled the ministry of making God’s love known - touching the hearts and lives of a multitude of people, especially her fellow Sisters, with her faith and wit. At age 101, Sister answered God's 2nd call - this time to return to her eternal home.  May she rest in peace.

    



Mother Matilde Parisi, CSJB

+ January 20, 1909 - February 15, 2013  +
May she rest in peace. Amen.

 

"Everyone has a story!"  For 104 year old Sister Matilde Parisi, hers was a war story. A World War II story of allying herself with an American major to face down the British commander  as she protected and provided for her children - the orphans entrusted to her care in their small, southern Italian town - while the German army advanced through their homeland.

     The Allies had secured the area near Salerno, Italy and US Major Canady arrived to serve as Military Governor. Among the many people seeking his assistance came a group of Sisters of St. John the Baptist led by "Madre Matilde". She was distraught because earlier that day his counterpart, the British Town Major, had ordered the Sisters and their charges to evacuate their building so that soldiers could be housed there.  Whenever Mother related the story, one could still

still glimpse the steadfast determination with which she presented her case to the Major. She was not worried about the Sisters - they could take care of themselves. But the children? The Sisters could not, would not abandon them to the streets.

 The Major agreed, but normal means of authority and diplomacy did not resolve the situation. So, encouraging the Sisters to invoke "a higher authority" , Major Canady invoked the authority of American General Mark Clark and the evacuation was forestalled.  The Sisters made sure that the townspeople knew what the Major had done for them - and he became a local hero. As the war kept on the townspeople found food for his men and miraculously an old American Buick for his own transportation!  Soon the war effort called the Major to serve in other arenas. After the war, Sr. Matilde and the Major never saw one another again, but for many, many years they kept up a warm correspondence. And to this day Sr. Matilde has never ceased remembering him in her prayers.

     It was with that same gentle but determined spirit that Sr. Matilde gave 86 years of loving commitment and service in religious life. Seventy of these years were dedicated to the needs of the American people, mostly young children in the urban areas of New York-New Jersey. And also to her Baptistine Sisters through a variety of leadership positions within the Province. Sr. Matilde's "war story" is also a story of Divine Providence. In the most unlikely circumstances God's loving care blossoms within our human situation through the goodness of others. 

 


Sister Veronica Di Santo, C.S.JB. 

+ March 9, 1911 - November 30, 2012  +
May she rest in peace. Amen.

 

1942

1963

1999

 

Sister Veronica Di Santo, C.S.JB. a member of the Sisters of St. John the Baptist, died peacefully in her sleep on Friday, November 30, 2012 at Mt. St. John Convent, Purchase, NY. The middle child of eight sisters and brothers, Sister Veronica Di Santo, C.S.JB. was born in Astoria, NY on March 9, 1911. Her first encounters with the Sisters of St. John the Baptist occurred as she took her young cousins to Our Lady of Loreto Day Nursery in lower Manhattan. So impressed was she by the Sisters’ prayerful commitment to God and their loving dedication to the children, especially the young orphans, and although still in her early teen years, she heard God’s call and determined to become a Sister. She entered the Sisters’ novitiate at St. John Villa Convent in 1927. In addition to her religious formation, Sister completed a B.S. in Education / English and an M.A. in History, both from Seton Hall University, South Orange, NJ

In l929 her first assignment as a professed Sister was to St. Lucy’s Convent, Newark, NJ, an orphanage and school owned by the Sisters.  She taught there until her transfer to St. John Villa Academy, Staten Island in 1942. During her twenty-one years at St. John Villa Academy (1942-1963) Sr. Veronica taught in and was principal of the Elementary and High Schools and also served as local Convent Superior. With the cooperation of her Faculty Sr. Veronica worked hard to broaden the academic curriculum. Her signature achievement was the integration of a performing arts program into the scholastic program which helped to promote the musical talents of many young girls some of whom went on to professional careers in the music industry. To this day alumnae fondly remember the school concerts staged at Carnegie Hall in New York City and in the Mosque Theater in Newark, NJ.

With the support of the newly established Parent/Teacher Association Sr Veronica directed efforts to expand the school facilities in order to accommodate an increasing enrollment of high school students. She oversaw fundraising for a new high school building known as Spellman Court, in honor of then Francis Cardinal Spellman. 

In 1963, Sister was transferred to Gloversville, NY to assume the educational leadership of Bishop Burke H.S. a co-ed high school sponsored by the Diocese of Albany. After completing 6 years as principal, in 1969 Sr. Veronica was assigned to the faculty of Alphonsus Junior College, Woodcliff Lake, NJ.

In 1973, with continuing enthusiasm for the young and their education, Sr. Veronica left the United States for Toronto, Canada and undertook the opening of Dante Alighieri High School, the first apostolic work for the Sisters of St. John the Baptist in Canada.  In 1985  Sr. Veronica was appointed to membership on the Congregation’s International Educational Committee. In this capacity she was asked by the General Government of  the Sisters of Saint John the Baptist (Rome, Italy) to go to the Philippines, where the Sisters had opened their first mission in the Asian region, and undertake the preliminary work needed to open schools there.   In 1995 needing greater physical care, Sister Veronica retired to Mt. St. John Convent, Purchase, NY where she remained until her death on November 30, 2012.

The legacy of this remarkable, international educator is witnessed in the establishment of solid academic programs, in the construction of new school buildings, but most of all in the lives of hundreds of students who have been shaped by her caring and her vision. Whether introducing young girls to the rhythm of religious life, assisting teachers and students in mastering the challenges of the classroom, or orchestrating the smooth running of convents and schools, the question Sister always put to herself was: "Do I impart goodness? Do I help them to see and know the love of God at work in their lives?"
